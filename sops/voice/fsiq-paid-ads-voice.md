# FSIQ Paid Ads Voice Guide — Video Ad Scripts
**Version:** 1.0 | **Last updated:** May 2026
**Source:** FSIQ ad scripts (AD-07, AD-09, Dollar Saved, Rob Tier, High Ticket Podcast, Success Rate)
  + Restaurant Unstoppable podcast transcript (Neil Chand, Speaker C)
**See also:** `sops/fsiq-brand-voice-paid-ads.md` (non-negotiable rules + format structure)
**Loaded by:** script-generator, humanizer skill (paid-ads context)

This guide is focused on the *voice calibration* layer of paid ad scripts —
how Neil sounds as a person, not the structural formatting rules (which live in
`fsiq-brand-voice-paid-ads.md`). When the two conflict, the brand voice SOP wins on rules;
this guide wins on natural-sounding delivery and language.

---

## 1. The Core Voice Model

Neil in a paid ad is not performing. He is explaining something he genuinely knows to someone
who would benefit from knowing it. The mental model:

> A guy who spent decades inside the largest food distributors in the country, now on the
> operator's side, who can't help but tell them how the system actually works.

He is not:
- A salesman with a quota
- A motivational speaker
- A tech founder pitching disruption

He speaks with quiet authority, not excitement.

---

## 2. Sentence Rhythm (Calibrated from Transcript)

Neil's natural speech pattern alternates between short landing sentences and longer explanatory
ones. He never fires five long sentences in a row, and he never fires five short ones.

Example rhythm from winning scripts:
> "I just left a meeting with a restaurant owner who spends more than $2M per year on food
> and was pretty sure his food costs were dialed in. [long — setup]
> He wasn't. [short — punch]
> When we ran the audit, we found $264,000 in recoverable savings. [long — proof]
> That's not unusual. [short — normalize]
> We see it across most of the operators we work with." [long — scale]

This alternation is what gives the scripts forward momentum without choppy staccato.

---

## 3. Opening Line Calibration

The best-performing scripts open in media res — inside a story, not announcing a story.

**In-media-res opening patterns (from winning scripts):**
> "I just left a meeting with a restaurant owner who spends more than $2M per year on food
> and was pretty sure his food costs were dialed in." *(AD-09 iPhone hook)*

> "A few months ago I was talking to a restaurant owner in [city] who told me he had the best
> pricing he could get from his distributor." *(case study opener)*

**What never opens a script:**
- Signposting: "In the next 2 minutes, I'm going to show you..."
- Questions: "Are you struggling with food costs?"
- Self-introduction: "Hi, I'm Neil Chand, CEO of FoodServiceIQ..."
- Upbeat preamble: "I'm really excited to share this with you today..."

---

## 4. Ellipsis Usage (Paid Ads Only)

Ellipses represent Neil's natural conversational pause in video scripts. They are correct
in paid ad scripts and should be preserved by the humanizer. This is the ONLY channel
where ellipses are the preferred pause marker.

✅ Paid ads: "No changes to your distributors… and no upfront cost."
❌ LinkedIn: "No changes to your distributors. And no upfront cost." (periods, not ellipses)

---

## 5. CTA Calibration

The paid ad CTA is low pressure, slightly casual, slightly conversational. It never sounds
like it was written by a marketing team.

**Winning CTA patterns (from real scripts):**
> "Click the link below… there's a free case study showing exactly how we saved one of our
> clients $271,000 per year on their food costs with literally no changes to ingredients or
> suppliers. So, yeah, check it out." *(Success Rate Ad)*

> "So check out the link below… it's completely free… so yeah, check it out."

**CTA rules:**
- "So yeah, check it out" is a signature. Keep it exactly.
- Never urgency ("Act fast," "Only X spots left").
- Never polished ("Don't miss this opportunity").
- The CTA should feel like the natural end of a conversation, not a closer.

---

## 6. Forbidden Language in Scripts

These words and constructions are never in Neil's paid ad voice:

| Forbidden | Why |
|-----------|-----|
| em dashes (—) | Breaks script rhythm; use ellipsis |
| "leverage" as a verb | Corporate. Neil doesn't use it. |
| "transformational," "pivotal," "groundbreaking" | AI vocabulary. |
| "empowering independent restaurants" | PR framing, not operator voice. |
| Math out loud | "5% of $2M is $100,000" — never. State results only. |
| Adversarial distributor framing | "your distributor is ripping you off" — never. |
| Downstream outcomes | New locations, bonuses, revenue growth — never. |
| Playbook page count | "50-page playbook" — never hardcode. |

## 7. Podcast Transcript Evidence (Restaurant Unstoppable — Speaker C)

These are confirmed patterns from Neil's recorded voice. Use as calibration reference
when rewriting or reviewing paid ad scripts.

**How Neil opens an explanation (restating first, then going):**
> "Well, in one sense, what we do is we help independent restaurants specifically reduce
> their food costs by an average of 5 to 7% on a 100 performance based basis."
Scripts should avoid this exact phrasing (too slow for an ad open) but should match the
directness of the answer — no preamble, just the substance.

**Natural connectors from transcript (approved in scripts):**
"and so", "you know", "I think", "certainly", "effectively", "I mean"
"moreover", "furthermore", "notably" — never Neil. Not once in the transcript.

**How Neil uses numbers:**
- Facts: stated directly — "5 to 7%", "$180,000", "18%"
- Estimates: hedged — "around 40 bucks a case", "maybe 20 locations"
Scripts should match this: specific facts stated flat, estimates qualified with "around" or
"roughly." Never round a specific fact to sound simpler.

**His FSIQ description (approved script language):**
> "it's capturing as much of that hidden margin as possible in favor of independent restaurants"
This phrasing is approved for use verbatim or near-verbatim in scripts.

**Three-phase structure (use for "how it works" script sections):**
> "distributor side optimization, manufacturer side — the back end of the invoice — and then
> auditing and maintaining all that work over time"

**Human moments to keep in scripts:**
> "To be clear, I'm not a restaurant owner, but…" (Neil says this in the podcast — use in scripts
> where it's authentic to acknowledge his operator-ally, not operator-identity position)
Scripts that include this kind of self-aware qualifier perform better than ones that don't.

**Energy level:**
Neil's podcast delivery is calm, measured, occasionally self-deprecating. He laughs at a grammar
joke. He talks about his fiancée. He admits he forgot what GPO stands for. Scripts should carry
this energy — unhurried, confident, human — not energized sales pitch energy.
