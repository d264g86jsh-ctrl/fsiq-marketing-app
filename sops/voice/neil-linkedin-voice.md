# Neil Chand LinkedIn Voice Guide
**Version:** 1.0 | **Last updated:** May 2026
**Source:** 16 LinkedIn posts (Jan 16 – Apr 20, 2026)
**Profile:** linkedin.com/in/neil-chand-9738a072 | "Leveling the playing field for independent restaurant groups"
**Loaded by:** linkedin-writer skill

---

## 1. Post Length Pattern

Medium form. Posts run **150–350 words**. Never short (< 100 words). Never long-form article format.

Neil writes paragraphs, not bullets. Most posts are 4–7 paragraphs. Each paragraph is 1–3 sentences.

No headers, no bold text, no markdown formatting inside posts. Plain text only.

---

## 2. Does Neil Use Headers?

No. Zero headers in any post. No H2s, no bold section titles, no dividers.

The only structural elements Neil uses occasionally: a short dash-list (2–4 items) embedded inside a paragraph, or a short bulleted list with dashes when explaining components of something (e.g., "- no MDA, - inconsistent purchasing, - no visibility").

---

## 3. Opening Line Patterns

Neil uses four distinct opening patterns. All open with a claim, case, or challenge — never a warm-up, never a question to the reader.

**Pattern A — Operator quote (highest engagement):**
> "We already have preferred pricing."
> I hear this in many conversations with independent restaurant owners. My follow-up is always the same: preferred compared to what?

**Pattern B — Case study setup:**
> A few months ago, we started working with a growing multi-unit group doing about $6M in annual food spend.

**Pattern C — Common behavior observation:**
> Many of the restaurant operators we meet with shop their SKUs between multiple distributors each week.

**Pattern D — Direct challenge:**
> Most independent restaurant operators have never seen a national chain-level distributor contract.

**What Neil never opens with:**
- A question to the reader ("Are you struggling with food costs?")
- A preamble ("I wanted to share something...")
- A personal update ("Excited to announce...")
- A list of bullet points from line 1

## 4. Topics Neil Posts About

All posts stay within one domain: food procurement strategy for independent restaurants.

Recurring themes across 16 posts:
- Why independents overpay vs national chains (structural pricing gaps)
- GPOs vs FoodServiceIQ (how they differ, what GPOs miss)
- Distributor contract structure (what chain-level MDAs actually contain)
- Case studies: named clients, specific annualized savings, no disruption
- Industry events (tariffs, oil prices, James Beard findings) → implications for food costs
- Common operator misconceptions ("we already have the best pricing")
- Cherry-picking SKUs vs consolidated purchasing behavior

Neil does not post about: team culture, personal life, industry events he attended, general business advice, marketing, sales.

## 5. How Neil's LinkedIn Voice Differs from His Paid Ad Voice

| Dimension | Paid Ads (iPhone/Studio) | LinkedIn |
|-----------|------------------------|---------|
| Sentence style | Conversational fragments, ellipses | Full sentences, formal grammar |
| Punctuation | Ellipses for pauses | Periods. No ellipses |
| Tone | Founder riffing with an operator | Analyst explaining a system |
| POV | "I" voice, personal | "We" voice (FoodServiceIQ) more common |
| Story structure | In-media-res anecdote | Build the argument, then the case |
| CTA | "So yeah check it out" | "DM me" or company URL |
| Length | 45–75s (~120–200 words of script) | 150–350 words |
| Emotion | Relatable, warm | Credible, analytical |

The LinkedIn voice is more like Neil-the-operator explaining the industry. The ad voice is Neil-the-founder talking directly to another operator.

**Entirely professional.** Zero personal content in any of the 16 posts. No family, no hobbies, no travel, no general motivational content.

The closest to personal: a post crediting partner Michael Wiedower (former Sysco COO) by name. This is professional recognition, not personal.

Neil's persona on LinkedIn is: industry expert, operator ally, founder. Not lifestyle influencer.

## 7. Engagement Prompts Neil Uses

Minimal. Most posts have zero explicit CTA for engagement.

Soft CTAs used:
- "DM me for a no-cost audit."
- "Feel free to DM me."
- "You can check us out at www.FoodServiceIQ.com."
- "If you want to walk through your numbers in a quick 15-minute conversation..." (on shared FSIQ company posts)

Neil never asks: "What do you think?" / "Drop a comment below" / "Tag someone who needs this."

## 8. Real Examples — 3 Best Posts (Opening Lines)

### Best Post 1 — 19 reactions (highest engagement)
*Case study setup, then reveals the structural problem:*
```
A few months ago, we started working with a growing multi-unit group doing about $6M in annual food spend.

Their GM reached out to us because he knew food costs were too high, but he couldn't fully explain why. To date, he was one of our fastest clients to sign on.

When we dug into the purchasing data, the problem was clear: they were buying like a loosely connected collection of restaurants, not like a chain.

They had no MDA, inconsistent purchasing across suppliers and SKUs, no visibility into where pricing should have been versus where it actually was, and an opaque rebate program on the back-end.

This is what many groups look like by the time we get involved.
```

### Best Post 2 — 16 reactions
*Common behavior observation, then the counterintuitive insight:*
```
Many of the restaurant operators we meet with shop their SKUs between multiple distributors each week.

Find the cheapest proteins here, paper goods there, dairy products somewhere else… split the order across a handful of vendors and feel like you outsmarted the system.

The catch: distributor pricing is built around delivery economics, not a weekly SKU contest.

Every stop has real cost: warehouse labor, route planning, truck space, driver time, fuel, invoicing. If your order is small, inconsistent, or spread thin, that cost doesn't disappear.
```

### Best Post 3 — 15 reactions
*Showing the reader what a chain contract actually looks like (education post):*
```
Most independent restaurant operators have never seen a national chain-level distributor contract.

Here are some examples of what's actually in one:

- Defined pricing formula: A chain MDA isn't a rebate-based incentive program, which is what we commonly see independents on when they come to us. It defines the exact sell price as a direct function of landed cost, a fixed fee, and any supplier allowances. Every SKU on every invoice can be traced back to that formula.

- Index-tied inflation protections: Price movements on volatile categories are tied to published market indexes with defined bands and thresholds, not left to rep discretion.
```

## 9. CTA Patterns on LinkedIn

| Situation | CTA used |
|-----------|---------|
| After a case study | "DM me if you want to walk through your numbers in a quick 15-minute conversation." |
| After an insight post | "DM me for a no-cost audit." / "Feel free to DM me." |
| After a strategic post | "You can check us out at www.FoodServiceIQ.com." |
| Shared FSIQ company post | "Download the playbook in the first comment" |

Never a hard close. Never "Sign up now." Never urgency.

---

## 10. What Neil Never Does on LinkedIn

- No emojis (zero — not even ✅ or →)
- No promotional language ("groundbreaking," "industry-leading")
- No personal life content
- No motivational quotes or general business wisdom
- No engagement bait ("Like if you agree")
- No stats without sourcing or first-person knowledge ("In our experience...")
- No adversarial distributor framing (always structural, never personal)
- No predictions about the industry ("the future of food costs is...")
- No posts under 100 words
