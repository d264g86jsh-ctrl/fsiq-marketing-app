## 1. The Core Tone Principle

> **"A smart operator talking to another smart operator."**
> Direct and confident but never salesy or polished.
> Casual and conversational but professional.
> The viewer should feel like they're getting a tip from someone who knows the industry — not a pitch from someone who wants their money.

Neil is not a restaurant owner. He says so explicitly in several winning ads. But he speaks from genuine industry expertise. The credibility comes from the depth of knowledge, not from pretending to share the audience's experience.

## 2. Non-Negotiable Writing Rules

These rules come directly from the creative team and have been validated through the scripting process. **Every rule applies to every script, no exceptions.**

### ❌ No Em Dashes
Use ellipses (`…`) for pauses and connective tissue. Never use `—` in scripts.

- ❌ `We've helped over 2,000 restaurants — and we're not done.`
- ✅ `We've helped over 2,000 restaurants… and we're not done.`

### ❌ No Short Choppy Sentences
Scripts must have momentum and flow. Each sentence should carry the next one forward. Staccato delivery is not Neil's voice.

- ❌ `We save money. It's real. No catches. Check it out.`
- ✅ `The savings are real, there are no catches, and you can check it out at the link below.`

### ❌ No "Not This / Not That" Constructions
Find the positive framing. Negative stacking sounds defensive.

- ❌ `There's no upfront cost, no changes to your distributors, and no strings attached.`
- ✅ `It's completely performance-based, you keep your existing distributors and ingredients, and there are no strings attached.`

### ❌ No Math Done Out Loud
Never walk the viewer through arithmetic in the script.

- ❌ `If you spend $2 million on food and we save you 5%, that's $100,000.`
- ✅ `Restaurants spending $2 million on food are often saving $100,000 or more annually.`

### ❌ No Adversarial or Fear-Mongering Language
The goal is invitation, not alarm. Never frame the pitch as the viewer losing to a competitor or being a victim of distributors.

- ❌ `Your distributor is ripping you off.`
- ✅ `Most independent restaurants are leaving significant money on the table in food costs — and they have no idea.`

- ❌ `Your competitors are getting better pricing than you.`
- ✅ `National chains consistently get better pricing on the same SKUs — and independent restaurants can access that same pricing.`

- ❌ `You're leaking money every month.`
- ✅ `There's likely room to save more than you'd expect on your current food costs.`

### ❌ No Downstream Outcome Framing
Don't lead with what clients do with the money (open new restaurants, invest in growth). Lead with the savings themselves. Downstream framing has been tested and noted as not working.

- ❌ `Our clients open an average of 2.2 new restaurant locations while working with us.`
- ✅ `We've helped over 2,000 restaurants save 5–7% on their annual food costs.`

### ❌ Never Hardcode Playbook Page Count
- ❌ `Download our free 50-page playbook`
- ❌ `Get our free 200-page playbook`
- ✅ `Download our free playbook`
- ✅ `Get a completely free playbook`

The landing page owns the page count detail. Scripts should never specify it.

## 3. Format Structure

Every FSIQ paid ad follows this structure:

```
[HOOK] → [BODY] → [CTA]
```

### Hook (first 3–5 seconds)
The hook determines scroll-stop rate. It must:
- Open **in media res** — already delivering value or already in the middle of a thought
- Never start with an introduction ("Hi, I'm Neil from FoodServiceIQ")
- Establish the relevant qualification quickly (revenue threshold, food spend, or restaurant type)
- Be self-qualifying — viewers outside the target should feel free to scroll

### Body
- Explain the **two mechanisms**: buying power ($2B+) + insider knowledge (distributor executive founders)
- Keep it moving — no long explanations, no lists, no numbered tactics read out loud
- Use a case study or specific example when the hook is general, and a general proof point when the hook leads with a specific example
- Should feel like Neil is **thinking out loud** or **sharing an insight**, not presenting a case

### CTA
- Always low-pressure and permission-giving
- Default: eBook/playbook download (LP2-EB) for cold traffic
- Case study (LP1-CS) for warmer audiences or when hook leads with specific result
- Phone call CTA for solution-aware or retargeting audiences
- Never end with urgency tactics, scarcity, or pressure language

## 4. Format Variants

### iPhone Format
- Neil films on iPhone, UGC-style, casual setting
- Hook: **riffing, loose, off-the-cuff** — like Neil is mid-thought or just got back from a meeting
- Best for cold traffic; feels organic
- Opens at 1:1 or 9:16 ratio
- Caption-dependent — viewers often watch without sound

**iPhone hook pattern (canonical):**
> "I just left a meeting with a restaurant owner who spends more than $2 million per year on food… he was sure his food costs were dialed in…"
> "I just got some interesting data back from our operations team…"
> "Okay, so I'm going to let you in on something…"

### Studio Format
- Filmed in a proper studio with desk, plants, books visible
- Hook: **slightly more composed than iPhone**, but still conversational and confident
- Zoom-ins used on key phrases and numbers
- Side angle used during "hold and point" moments (when holding up the playbook or pointing to data)
- Multiple angles — front and side cuts alternate for visual interest

### Podcast Format
- Interview/podcast aesthetic; Neil speaking directly to camera as if mid-conversation
- Hook: often opens with a large-scale client example or surprising outcome
- Most natural for the "2,000+ restaurants, $20M+ groups" credibility stories
- Highest performer format for cold traffic (AD-28 is #1 performer; AD-07 is #5)

### Testimonial Format
- Client on camera or client call audio played over Neil's reaction
- Hook: lead with the client's exact words or audio clip
- Body: Neil provides context, savings %, client scale
- Best with audio clip hook: "This is one of the best calls I've had in 20 years in the business" — Spirits client

## 5. Hook Taxonomy

The following hook types have been used and tested. The top performers are marked.

| Hook Type | Description | Example | Performance |
|-----------|-------------|---------|-------------|
| **Post-meeting / insider** | Neil just left a meeting and has something to share | "I just left a meeting with a restaurant owner who spends $2M/yr on food…" | ✅ AD-09 — #4 overall, best iPhone ad |
| **Podcast/social proof opener** | Leads with a large client or surprising outcome | "Even restaurant groups spending $20M/yr on food — we're still saving them 4–6%…" | ✅ AD-07 — #5 overall |
| **Gift/offer reveal** | Positions playbook or case study as a gift | "The best gift I could give you this year is something we normally charge for…" | ✅ AD-23 (#2), AD-18 (#3) |
| **Blurred book / prop-driven** | Physical prop creates curiosity; hook reveals what it is | "If your restaurant does $3M+, I'd like to send you this book completely free…" | ✅ AD-28 — #1 overall |
| **Data hook** | Opens with a surprising internal data point | "I just got data from our ops team — our typical client opens 1.2 new restaurants while working with us" | Tested; downstream framing concern noted |
| **Pattern interrupt / fail hook** | Subverts expectation; leads with failure or "catch" | "Let me tell you about the times FoodServiceIQ completely failed our clients…" | AD-22 (Success Rate) — tested |
| **Self-qualifying filter** | Opens by excluding viewers who aren't a fit | "If you do under $3M per year in revenue, this isn't for you — you can skip this" | AD-21/AD-27 tiers — tested |
| **Invoice / proof hook** | Physical evidence creates credibility | "I have two restaurant owners, same city, same supplier — one paying 30% more. I have the invoices here to prove it." | AD-32 — tested, newer |
| **Announcement / exclusivity** | Positions a new development as exciting news | "Something we've wanted to do for a long time just became possible…" | AD-33 — tested, newer |

## 6. CTA Rules

### Default CTA (LP2-EB — cold traffic)
```
So check out the link below… there's a completely free playbook there showing the exact 
strategies we've used to help 2,000+ restaurants save 5–7% on their food costs… 
and if you like what you see, feel free to schedule a quick call with our team 
and we can let you know if it's a good fit.
```

**Key CTA characteristics:**
- Always opens with "check out the link below" or "click the link below"
- Describes what they get (the playbook / case study), not what they must do
- Ends with a low-pressure call offer ("feel free to…", "if you like what you see…")
- Never: "Sign up now," "Limited spots available," "Don't miss out"
- Never: "Schedule a call" as the primary CTA on cold traffic ads

### Case Study CTA (LP1-CS — warmer or high-ticket)
```
So click the link below… there's a free case study there showing exactly how we 
saved one of our clients $271,000 per year on their food costs — with literally 
no changes to ingredients or suppliers… so yeah, check it out.
```

### Phone Call CTA (retargeting / solution-aware)
```
So if that sounds interesting, check out the link below… you can find out exactly 
how this works, and if you like what you see, feel free to schedule a quick 
10-minute call with our team and we can let you know if it's a good fit.
```

### CTA Tone Signature
The best CTAs end with something slightly casual — "so yeah, check it out" or "so… yeah… check it out." This is intentional. It punctures any remaining sales pressure and signals confidence without desperation.

### ❌ NEVER Hardcode Playbook Page Count
See Non-Negotiable Rules above. Use "our free playbook" or "a completely free playbook" only.

## 7. Awareness Level Targeting

| Awareness Level | What they know | Hook style | CTA |
|----------------|----------------|-----------|-----|
| **Unaware** | Don't know they're overpaying | Gift/offer, pattern interrupt, announcement | LP2-EB |
| **Problem Aware** | Know food costs are high, don't know solution | Post-meeting, social proof, data hook | LP2-EB or LP1-CS |
| **Solution Aware** | Know FSIQ or similar services exist | Self-qualifying filter, tier ad | LP1-CS or Phone Call |
| **Most Aware** | Ready to buy or retargeting | Direct offer, exclusivity | Phone Call |

## 8. The No-Disruption Guarantee (Always Include)

Every script — regardless of format, hook type, or CTA — must communicate:

> **No changes to ingredients, distributors, or suppliers.**

This is the primary objection handler and must appear in the body or CTA of every ad. The canonical phrasing:
- "without any changes to your ingredients or suppliers"
- "no changes to your distributors or SKUs"
- "you keep your existing vendor relationships"

Never omit this. It is load-bearing for conversion.

---

## 9. The Performance-Based Model (Always Include)

Every ad body or CTA should communicate:
- Zero upfront cost
- 100% performance-based
- If no savings found, client pays nothing

Canonical phrasing:
- "it's 100% performance-based — there's zero cost up front"
- "if we can't find you savings, you don't owe us anything"
- "we only share in a small percentage of the savings we secure"

## 10. Script Length Guidelines

| Format | Optimal length | Notes |
|--------|---------------|-------|
| iPhone cold traffic | 30–60 seconds | Under 60s outperforms; punchier the better |
| Studio / Podcast cold traffic | 45–90 seconds | 60–75s sweet spot |
| High Ticket VSL | 90–120 seconds | Only for $10M+ ad sets |
| Short Ad (hyper-HP) | 20–35 seconds | Special production; not default |

## 11. Variation Requirements

When generating scripts, **always produce multiple hook variations:**
- Minimum 2 hooks per concept
- One hook for iPhone delivery (looser, riffing, off-the-cuff)
- One hook for Studio delivery (more composed, same message)
- Body and CTA are shared across iPhone and Studio versions

**Hook variation guidance:**
- Vary the opening framing, not the core message
- Each hook should feel like a different entry point to the same story
- If stuck, offer line-level options rather than full rewrites

## 12. What NOT to Write (Compiled from Doc + History)

| Avoid | Use instead |
|-------|------------|
| "Your distributor is ripping you off" | "Most independents are overpaying, and they don't know it" |
| "Your competitors are paying less" | "National chains get better pricing — and you can access that same pricing" |
| "You're losing $X every month" | "There's likely $X or more in savings in your current food costs" |
| Math done out loud ($2M × 5% = $100k) | State the result directly ("restaurants saving $100k+") |
| "50-page playbook" / "200-page playbook" | "our free playbook" |
| "Don't miss this" / "Limited spots" | "Check it out… no strings attached" |
| Em dashes | Ellipses |
| "Not A, not B, not C" | Positive reframe of each point |
| Downstream outcomes (new locations, team bonuses) | The savings number itself |
| Jackson-style third-party host | Neil or Rob only |
| VSL-style long intro | Open in media res, no warmup |

## 13. Proof Points Ranked by Persuasion Strength

Use in this order when constructing body copy:

1. **Specific client savings** — MaryAnn's $264k, Cincinnati $520k, Spirits 12%
2. **Scale** — 2,000+ restaurants, $2B+ buying power
3. **Founder credential** — former COO of Sysco, President of PFG
4. **Performance model** — zero upfront, pay on results only
5. **No disruption** — keep your distributors and ingredients
6. **Availability** — now open to $5M+ restaurants (exclusivity/expansion angle)
